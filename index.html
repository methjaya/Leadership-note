Lesson 1 — The Nature of Leadership

Definition of Leadership

"Leadership is the influencing process of leaders and followers to achieve organizational objectives through change." — Lussier & Achua (2016)

Key idea: Leadership is a process (not a trait), it involves influence, it requires followers, and it aims at change.

4 Elements of Leadership: "PIFC" — Process · Influence · Followers · Change

Leadership vs Management — MUST KNOW

DimensionLeadershipManagement
FocusChange & innovationStability & efficiency
BasisInfluence & inspirationAuthority & rules
PeopleMotivates followersDirects subordinates
DirectionSets vision & goalsPlans, organizes, controls
Question"What should we do?""How do we do it?"
RiskChallenges the status quoMaintains status quo
Famous quote"Do the right things""Do things right"
Both are needed. Great organizations need managers AND leaders. Some people are both.

Myths about Leadership (common exam sub-question)

  • Leadership can't be learned — FALSE: Leadership skills can be developed
  • Leaders are only at the top — FALSE: Leadership at all levels
  • Leaders are born by title — FALSE: Leadership is a process, not a position
  • Great leaders work alone — FALSE: Collaboration is key
  • Failure is not an option — FALSE: Failure is part of learning
  • Leaders only give orders — FALSE: Leaders inspire, listen, and coach

Leadership Skills (Lussier & Achua)

Technical Skills
Specific knowledge of how to do the job. "Things" focus.
Interpersonal Skills
Ability to work with and through people. "People" focus.
Decision-Making Skills
Conceptual skills, problem solving, strategic thinking. "Ideas" focus.

Lesson 2 — Trait & Behavioural Theories

Trait Theory — Key Traits of Leaders

Northouse's 5 Traits: "DISCO"
Determination · Intelligence · Sociability · Confidence · Integrity (+ Openness to Experience)
Lussier & Achua's Traits: "DISHES FI"
Dominance · Intelligence · Self-confidence · High Energy · Integrity · Sensitivity to others · Emotional Stability · Flexibility · Internal Locus of Control
4 Traits of Great Leaders (easy to recall): "VICE"
Vision · Integrity · Courage · Effectiveness (in building others up)

Big Five Personality Model (OCEAN)

OCEAN = Openness · Conscientiousness · Extraversion · Agreeableness · Neuroticism (Emotional Stability)
TraitHigh =
OpennessWilling to try new things, creative, adaptable
ConscientiousnessDependable, organized, goal-oriented
ExtraversionSociable, assertive, energetic (good for leadership)
AgreeablenessCooperative, trusting, gets along with people
Emotional Stability (low Neuroticism)Calm, resilient under stress (good for leadership)

Criticisms of Trait Theory

  • No definitive list of leadership traits agreed upon
  • Fails to take situations into account
  • Not useful for training — traits are hard to teach
  • Subjective — who decides which traits matter most?

Behavioural Theories

Ohio State Model
Two dimensions:
  • Initiating Structure — task-oriented, defines roles, sets goals
  • Consideration — people-oriented, builds relationships, shows care
Best leaders: HIGH on both
Michigan Model (also called Likert)
Two styles:
  • Job-centred — focus on tasks, standards, output
  • Employee-centred — focus on relationships, welfare, participation
Employee-centred = higher productivity long-term
Leadership Grid (Blake & Mouton)
  • Impoverished (1,1) — minimum effort on both
  • Authority-compliance (9,1) — task focus, people like machines
  • Country Club (1,9) — friendly, low performance pressure
  • Middle-of-Road (5,5) — adequate on both
  • Team Leader (9,9) — high task AND high people ⭐ IDEAL
Democratic vs Autocratic
Democratic leader's absence: Followers perform BETTER because they've been empowered to make decisions, take ownership, and have developed internal motivation. Autocratic followers depend entirely on the leader for direction.

Lesson 3 — Situational & Contingency Leadership

Hersey-Blanchard Situational Leadership ⭐ MOST TESTED MODEL

"Effective leaders need to be flexible, and must adapt themselves according to the situation."

Based on two factors: (1) Follower Readiness Level and (2) Leader's Style

Readiness Levels: "R1R2R3R4" = 'Unable-Unwilling' to 'Able-Willing'
Readiness LevelAbilityWillingnessBest Style
R1 — Unable & Insecure/UnwillingLowLowS1 — Telling/Directing
R2 — Unable but Willing/ConfidentLowHighS2 — Selling/Coaching
R3 — Able but Insecure/UnwillingHighLowS3 — Participating/Supporting
R4 — Able, Confident & WillingHighHighS4 — Delegating
Styles memory trick: "Tell, Sell, Support, Delegate" = T-S-S-D
S1=Tell (high task, low relationship) → S2=Sell (high task + high relationship) → S3=Support (low task, high relationship) → S4=Delegate (low task, low relationship)

What each style looks like

S1 — Telling (R1 follower)
One-way communication. Specific task directions. Close supervision. Simple and specific instructions. "Do this, by this date, this way."
S2 — Selling/Coaching (R2 follower)
Two-way dialogue. Leader-made decisions but explains why. Ask questions to clarify ability. Explain role. Supportive and persuasive.
S3 — Participating/Supporting (R3 follower)
Encourage input. Actively listen. Follower-made decisions. Two-way communication. Support risk-taking. Build confidence. Praise.
S4 — Delegating (R4 follower)
Follower-made decisions. Light supervision. Delegate tasks fully. Reinforce results. Leader stays out of the way. "My job is to stay out of your way."
Exam Q application: "Team members know their job well, willing to decide" = R4 → S4 Delegating. Enthusiastic but new = R2 → S2 Selling.

Fiedler's Contingency Model (LPC Scale)

LPC = Least Preferred Coworker score. Rate the coworker you find hardest to work with.

High LPC Score (≥64) → Relationship-motivated leader
Even rates difficult coworkers positively. Focuses on people and relationships. Performs best in intermediate-favorability situations.
Low LPC Score (≤57) → Task-motivated leader
Rates difficult coworkers very negatively. Focuses on getting the job done. Performs best in very favorable OR very unfavorable situations.

3 Situational Factors (determine favorability):

1. Leader-Member Relations
Good/Poor — degree of trust and respect followers have for leader. Most important factor.
2. Task Structure
Structured/Unstructured — how clearly defined the task is. Clear tasks = favorable.
3. Position Power
Strong/Weak — formal authority the leader has (ability to hire, fire, reward).
Favorability memory: "LTP" = Leader-member relations (most important) · Task structure · Position power
Past paper Q3 scenario: LPC score = 45 → LOW LPC = Task-motivated leader. Large resistance from employees = Poor leader-member relations. New external hire = low position power. Unstructured new policy = unstructured task. This is a very unfavorable situation → Task-motivated leader (low LPC) actually fits this situation!

Lesson 4 — Leader's Role in Achieving Competitive Advantage

What is Competitive Advantage?

The unique value proposition that allows an organization to outperform rivals — more market share, profitability, or customer loyalty. Answer to: "Why should customers choose us?"

Leaders are the catalysts that transform organizational potential into market-leading performance.

How Leaders Drive Competitive Advantage

1
Setting Strategic Direction & Vision
Articulate a compelling future state. Align organizational resources toward clear goals.
2
Building High-Performance Culture
Create culture of innovation, accountability, and excellence. Culture = invisible hand guiding decisions.
3
Developing & Retaining Talent
Identify and nurture leadership potential. Invest in employee development.
4
Driving Innovation
Encourage risk-taking, experimentation, and creative problem solving.
5
Execution Excellence
Translate strategy into action. Ensure resource optimization and organizational alignment.

Transformational Triangle (from 2019/20 Q4)

Organizational transformation requires alignment of three elements:

Strategy
Where the organization is going — new direction, new markets, new business model. What needs to change.
People
Who will implement — skills development, change management, engaging employees, addressing resistance.
Processes/Systems
How it gets done — new processes, IT systems, structures that support the new strategy.
For the software company Q (2019/20): Strategy = shift to high-end solutions. People = develop leadership skillsets. Processes = new governance and solution design workflows.

3 Psychological States for Employee Engagement (Hackman & Oldham)

"MRK" = Meaningfulness · Responsibility · Knowledge of results
Experienced Meaningfulness
Employees must feel the work matters. Leaders provide purpose and vision so employees see how their work contributes to bigger goals.
Experienced Responsibility
Employees feel accountable for their own outcomes. Leaders give autonomy and ownership over tasks.
Knowledge of Results
Employees get clear feedback on performance. Leaders provide timely, honest, constructive feedback.

Lesson 5 — Leadership Development

Leadership Development Strategies

Formal Training Programs
Workshops, seminars, online courses. Provide theoretical frameworks and practical skills.
Mentorship & Coaching
Pairing with experienced mentors for guidance, feedback, and career support.
Special Assignments / Stretch Assignments
High-challenge roles beyond current capability. Accelerates growth through real experience.
Job Rotation
Moving through different roles to build broad understanding and multiple skill sets.

Developing Leaders Through Special Assignments (from 2019/20 Q5iii)

  • Select meaningful challenges — assign tasks slightly beyond current ability to create "stretch"
  • Provide support & mentoring — leader stays available as a coach, not to do it for them
  • Set clear goals and expectations — define what success looks like
  • Allow freedom to fail — learning from mistakes is essential to development
  • Debrief and reflect — structured reflection after the assignment to extract lessons
  • Recognize and reward growth — acknowledge progress, not just results

Substitutes for Leadership (from 2019/20 Q5ii)

Situations where formal leadership is less needed or can be replaced:

  • Highly skilled/experienced employees — don't need direction (S4 followers)
  • Strong organizational culture — culture guides behavior without a leader
  • Clear, structured tasks — process itself provides direction
  • Self-managed teams — members collectively lead themselves
  • Advanced technology systems — automated processes reduce need for oversight
  • Professional norms — doctors, lawyers follow professional ethics, not just manager's orders

Lesson 6 — Followership

What is Followership?

"Followership is a discipline of supporting leaders and helping them lead well. It is not submission, but the wise and good care of leaders."

Followers determine whether a leader is actually effective. Without followers, there is no leader.

What followers do (5 roles): "GCSSL"
Get the job done · Care about the mission · Support the leader · Speak up to leaders (challenge) · Learn from leaders

LMX Theory — Leader-Member Exchange ⭐ 2023/24 Q1

Leader develops different quality relationships with each follower (vertical dyads). Not all followers are treated equally.

In-Group ("High LMX")
High trust, mutual respect, extra responsibility, autonomy, information sharing. Go above and beyond.
Sarala's example: Sithum & Malani
Mid-Group
Moderate exchange. Complete tasks adequately but limited extra engagement.
Sarala's example: Kalani & Sudesh
Out-Group ("Low LMX")
Formal, task-only communication. Basic contract only. Limited development opportunities.
Sarala's example: Priya (new member)

Positive consequences of LMX:

  • In-group members highly motivated → better performance for team
  • Leader allocates resources efficiently to reliable members

Negative consequences of LMX:

  • Out-group members feel excluded → disengagement, reduced morale
  • Perceived favoritism damages team cohesion
  • Lost potential from out-group members who are not developed

Kelly's 5 Followership Styles

"A PAC E" = Alienated · Passive · Effective · Conformist · Exemplary (Effective = same as Exemplary here)
StyleCritical ThinkingActivity LevelDescription
Exemplary/EffectiveHighActiveBest followers — independent thinkers who actively engage. NASA engineers. ⭐
AlienatedHighPassiveCritical but passive. Feel cheated. Question decisions but don't contribute solutions. Enron aftermath.
ConformistLowActiveYes-people. Do what they're told enthusiastically. Follow without questioning. Hitler's bunker subordinates.
PassiveLowPassiveDo only what's asked. No initiative. "Just following orders." Factory worker example.
PragmatistMediumMediumSurvive and play it safe. Middling on both dimensions. Avoid risk.
Courageous Follower (Chaleff) = challenges unsafe/unethical directives even in hierarchical organizations. Is both supportive AND willing to speak truth to power. "Healthy followership requires moral courage."

Lesson 7 — Authentic & Servant Leadership

Authentic Leadership

"Authentic leaders are leaders who know who they are, know what they believe in and value, and act on those values openly and candidly." — Robbins (2013)

Intrapersonal perspective
Focus on internal: self-knowledge, self-regulation, self-concept. The leader's inner world.
Interpersonal perspective
Focus on relationships: how leader interacts with followers transparently and honestly.

Journey of Authentic Leadership (4 phases):

1
Life Story
Formative experiences shape values. Childhood, family, early career. Who you are comes from where you've been.
2
Crucible (Testing)
Difficult period at work or home that tests you to the core. Crisis reveals character. "What doesn't kill you makes you stronger."
3
Leading
Accumulation of leadership experiences. Transformation from 'I' to 'We'.
4
Legacy
What you leave behind. How you've helped others grow.
Exam insight: "Being authentic is not enough — political acumen is also needed" (2019/20 Q5i). Authentic leaders need BOTH inner values AND the ability to navigate organizational politics, build coalitions, and influence strategically.

Servant Leadership (Robert Greenleaf, 1970) ⭐ 2023/24 Q2b

"Servant leadership is a contradiction to traditional leadership" — traditional leadership puts the leader first (leader→followers), servant leadership puts followers first (followers→leader).

Core of Servant Leadership: "Serve First" — The servant leader's first impulse is to serve, not to lead.

7 characteristics of servant leaders (model):

Conceptualizing
See the big picture while managing daily operations
Emotional Healing
Sensitive to follower wellbeing; create a safe space
Putting Followers First
Prioritize followers' needs before own interests
Helping Followers Grow
Develop followers' potential; mentorship focus
Behaving Ethically
Strong moral principles; do what is right
Empowering
Give followers authority and ownership
Example: Nelson Mandela — "At first I was afraid. But it's not about me, it's about the people." Humility, service to others, building others up.

Why it's a "contradiction": Traditional theory says leader is at the top of the hierarchy. Servant leadership inverts this — followers are at the top, leader supports from below. Control shifts from leader to follower (very high follower autonomy).

Ethical Leadership

  • Ethical egoism — create greatest good for self (self-interest)
  • Utilitarianism — create greatest good for greatest number
  • Altruism — promote best interests of others above self
Servant and ethical leadership overlap: both prioritize others over self. Leaders create organizational culture that is either ethical or toxic.

Lesson 8 — Charismatic, Transactional & Transformational Leadership

Charismatic Leadership

"Charismatic leaders are leaders who have the ability to inspire and motivate people to do more than they would normally do, despite obstacles and personal sacrifice."

5 Key Characteristics of Charismatic Leaders:

"VISU C" = Vision · Personal Risk · Sensitivity · Unconventional Behavior · Congeniality
  • Vision and Articulation — proposes a future better than status quo
  • Personal Risk — self-sacrifice to achieve vision
  • Sensitivity to follower needs — responsive and empathetic
  • Unconventional Behavior — does what traditional leaders wouldn't
  • Environmental sensitivity — reads context and adapts
Dark side: Charismatic leaders may let personal goals override organizational goals. Followers can become too dependent. Risk of cult-like following. Examples: Hitler, Mussolini.

Transactional vs Transformational — ⭐ MOST TESTED COMPARISON

DimensionTransactionalTransformational
BasisExchange relationship (reward/punishment)Inspiration and vision
MotivationContingent reward, management by exceptionIdealized influence, intellectual stimulation
FocusCurrent goals, meeting targetsFuture vision, change, growth
Leadership style"Carrot and stick" approachInspires beyond self-interest
Follower responseCompliance — do what's asked for rewardCommitment — go above and beyond
ChangeMaintains status quo, incrementalDrives major change, transformational
People treated asMeans to an endIndividuals with unique potential
Best forStable environments, clear tasksChange, crisis, turnaround situations
Transformational 4 I's: "IIII" = Idealized Influence · Inspirational Motivation · Intellectual Stimulation · Individualized Consideration

Full Range Leadership Model

Spectrum from most passive/ineffective to most active/effective:

Laissez-Faire → Management by Exception (passive) → Management by Exception (active) → Contingent Reward (Transactional) → Individualized Consideration → Intellectual Stimulation → Inspirational Motivation → Idealized Influence (Transformational)

Laissez-faire = most passive AND most ineffective. Idealized Influence = most active AND most effective.

Transactional leader in a crisis (2020/21 Q1a — Frank Zoysa)

Frank as transactional leader: Would establish clear performance agreements with followers. Set explicit targets for the new business model. Use reward/punishment to drive compliance. Communicate "if you hit these targets, you get this reward." Good for short-term alignment but may not inspire deep commitment to change.

Frank as transformational leader: Would create compelling vision of the new direction. Inspire followers emotionally. Intellectually stimulate them to think differently. Provide individualized support. Followers would commit to the change, not just comply. Better for a major turnaround.

Michigan Model recommendation: Employee-centred behavior. In a change situation with resistance, focusing on relationships, trust, and employee needs will unlock commitment better than pure task focus.

Lesson 9 — Leadership and Communication

Why Communication is Central to Leadership

  • Articulate vision and mission to followers
  • Motivate and inspire teams toward goals
  • Build trust and credibility
  • Manage conflict and resolve issues
  • Provide feedback and direction
Satya Nadella (Microsoft) and Jack Ma (Alibaba) — both built trust through open, inspiring communication. Leaders who listen well AND speak with clarity gain more follower commitment.

Storytelling as a Leadership Tool ⭐ Repeated Question

Why storytelling works: Stories bypass rational resistance and connect emotionally. Humans process narrative 22x better than facts alone.

How leaders use storytelling to persuade:

1
Create an emotional connection
Share relatable characters, struggles, and triumphs. Followers see themselves in the story.
2
Communicate vision through narrative
Instead of "we need 20% growth," tell a story of what the organization will look like in 5 years if they succeed.
3
Use personal vulnerability
Leaders who share their own failures and learning earn authenticity and trust.
4
Reinforce values through stories
Tell stories of employees who exemplified company values — shapes culture implicitly.
5
Make complex change feel possible
Stories of other organizations that made similar transitions reduce fear of change.
Story structure: "SCAR" = Situation · Conflict · Action · Result

How Communication Builds Leader Credibility

  • Consistency — doing what you say builds trust over time
  • Transparency — sharing both good and bad news honestly
  • Active listening — leaders who listen well are more trusted
  • Clarity — clear messages reduce confusion and anxiety
  • Empathy — acknowledging follower feelings increases connection
  • Two-way dialogue — seeking input shows respect and improves decisions

Lesson 10 — Team Leadership

Groups vs Teams

GroupsTeams
Work on a common goalFully committed to common goals they developed together
Accountable to managerMutually accountable to each other
No clear stable culture; frequent conflictsCollective culture; high cohesion
Individual performance focusTeam performance focus; synergy

Types of Teams

Functional Teams
Members from same department. Appointed leader with authority. Leader is task + relationship oriented.
Cross-Functional Teams
Members from different departments. Leader selected by top management. Needs interpersonal + position power.
Self-Managed Teams (SMTs)
No formal leader — team manages itself. High-skilled, autonomous members. External leader only handles what team can't.
Virtual Teams
Geographically dispersed. Technology-mediated. Need extra communication effort.

Self-Managed Teams (SMTs) — Distributed Leadership ⭐

In SMTs, leadership is distributed — all members share leadership responsibilities based on expertise and situation. No single formal leader within the team.

Relevance of Distributed Leadership in SMTs:

  • Faster decisions — no bottleneck waiting for one leader
  • Higher engagement — all members feel ownership
  • Leverages diverse expertise — right person leads for each task
  • Resilient — team functions even if one member is absent
  • Common in: agile software teams, medical teams, military units
External leader's role in SMTs: handles what the team can't (budgets, escalations, top management interface). Internal leadership rotates based on task needs.

Advantages & Dilemmas of Teamwork

Advantages
  • Achieve synergy (1+1=3)
  • Mutual support
  • Greater innovation
  • Higher job satisfaction
Dilemmas/Problems
  • Free riders — benefit without contributing
  • Social loafing — less effort in groups
  • Groupthink — no one challenges bad decisions
  • Lack of cooperation

Lesson 11 — Level 5 Leadership (Jim Collins)

What is Level 5 Leadership? ⭐ 2023/24 Q2a

"Level 5 leaders possess a paradoxical blend of professional will and personal humility." — Jim Collins, Harvard Business Review (2001)

Research: studied 11 companies that went from "Good to Great." The common factor was always a Level 5 leader at the top.

"Fierce Resolve + Deep Humility" = Level 5
They are quietly driven inside, but never self-promoting outside.

5 Leadership Levels (pyramid)

1
Highly Capable Individual
Makes productive contributions through talent, knowledge, skills.
2
Contributing Team Member
Contributes to group objectives. Works effectively with others.
3
Competent Manager
Organizes people and resources toward effective and efficient pursuit of objectives.
4
Effective Leader
Catalyzes commitment to and vigorous pursuit of a clear compelling vision. High performance standards.
5
Level 5 Executive
Builds enduring greatness through a paradoxical combination of personal humility PLUS professional will. The highest level.

Professional Will + Personal Humility — elaborated

Professional Will (Fierce Resolve)
  • Creates superb results; built to last
  • Demonstrates an unwavering resolve to do what must be done
  • Sets high standards and holds people to them
  • Takes responsibility for poor results — "looks in the mirror"
  • Makes painful decisions for long-term success (Darwin Smith sold Kimberly-Clark's mills!)
Personal Humility
  • Does not seek glory or fame
  • Never boastful; modest about achievements
  • Credits success to other people and luck — "looks out the window"
  • Quiet, calm determination — not charismatic or loud
  • Develops successors who are even better
Window and Mirror concept: Level 5 leaders look OUT THE WINDOW to credit others for success, look IN THE MIRROR to take responsibility for failure. Opposite of ego-driven leaders who look in the mirror for credit and out the window to blame others.

Lesson 12 — Power, Influence & Politics

Power, Influence & Authority — the difference

Power
Capacity to influence others' behavior or attitudes. Can exist without using it. "Potential influence."
Influence
The actual process of affecting behavior — changing someone's action or opinion. Power in action.
Authority
Power vested in a particular position (formal). You have authority because of your job title.
Power is "PSD": Situational · Relational · Dynamic (it changes)

Types of Power ⭐

Position power (hard) = "LRC C" — Legitimate · Reward · Coercive · Connection
Personal power (soft) = "REI" — Referent · Expert · Information
TypeSourceExampleOutcome
LegitimatePosition/titleManager tells employee to complete a taskCompliance
RewardAbility to give rewardsSalary increases, bonuses, promotionsCompliance
CoerciveAbility to punishDemotion, dismissal, disciplineResistance (negative)
ConnectionKnowing powerful people"I know the CEO personally"Compliance
ReferentRespect & admirationPeople follow because they admire the leaderCommitment
ExpertSpecialized knowledgeDoctor's medical adviceCommitment
InformationAccess to valued informationPerson who controls key dataCompliance
Why leaders use coercive power despite negative effects (2020/21 Q5b): It's fast, visible, creates fear-compliance, seems to work short-term. But expert and referent power (soft power) create genuine commitment and are sustainable. Coercive power creates resistance and damages relationships.

Cialdini's 6 Principles of Influence

"LSRCAS" = Liking · Social Proof · Reciprocity · Commitment & Consistency · Authority · Scarcity
Liking
People say yes to those they like.
Social Proof
"Everyone else is doing it."
Reciprocity
Return favors; feel obligated to give back.
Commitment
Consistent with past commitments.
Authority
Follow experts and legitimate figures.
Scarcity
Want what's rare or diminishing.

Organizational Politics

Politics = gaining and using power to obtain desired outcomes. Inevitable in organizations — where there is ambiguity and uncertainty, politics occurs.

Common political tactics (Allen et al. 1979):

  • Attacking/blaming others (54%)
  • Using information as a political tool (54%)
  • Creating a favourable image (53%) — impression management
  • Developing a base of support (37%)

2 strategies to mitigate negative politics (2023/24 Q3b):

  • Transparency & fairness — reduce ambiguity that creates political behavior; make decisions openly
  • Build a trust-based culture — when people trust the system is fair, they rely less on manipulation
Positive side of politics: building coalitions, networking, influencing policy — can drive legitimate organizational change.

Lesson 13 — Leaders as Culture Creators

What is Organizational Culture?

All of a company's beliefs, values, and attitudes, and how these influence the behaviour of its employees. Culture affects how it feels to work somewhere and how customers experience the organization.

Culture stat: 77% of workers consider culture before applying. 65% of employees stay because of culture.

Why Culture Matters to Leaders

  • Culture is the "invisible hand" — guides decisions when the leader isn't in the room
  • A competitive culture is hard to copy — sustainable advantage
  • Improves recruitment, retention, brand identity, performance, and innovation

Leader's Role in Culture Creation

  • Model the values — leaders must personally embody the culture they want
  • Foster collaboration — set examples of trust, respect, encourage team decisions
  • Create psychological safety — environment where people can speak up, take risks, fail safely
  • Recognize and reward culture-aligned behavior — what gets rewarded becomes culture
  • Communicate purpose clearly — connect daily work to organizational mission
  • Address culture-damaging behavior quickly — what leaders tolerate defines culture too

Power-Distance Culture Mismatch (2020/21 Q6b)

High Power-Distance Leader
Expects: followers defer to authority, decisions come from top, hierarchy is respected, formal communication.
Low Power-Distance Followers
Expect: challenge authority, participate in decisions, informal communication, flat structures.

Problems that arise:

  • Followers feel disrespected or suppressed when not consulted
  • Leader perceives followers as insubordinate or disrespectful
  • Miscommunication, conflict, low morale, high turnover

Strategies to resolve:

  • Cultural awareness training for both leader and followers
  • Leader adapts to more participative style (democratic shift)
  • Establish clear norms that respect both orientations

Lesson 14 — Crisis Leadership & Change Management

Crisis Leadership

The process a leader or team uses during an event that threatens the organization — planning, motivating, managing PR, and preserving the organization.

5 Characteristics of Effective Crisis Leaders: "DECES"
Decisive · Excellent communicator · Composed · Expert problem-solver · Empathetic

Stages of a Crisis

1
Pre-Crisis (Warning Stage)
Early warning signs appear. Smart leaders scan for threats and prepare.
2
Crisis Onset
Crisis erupts. Requires immediate action — assess, communicate, delegate.
3
Crisis Management
Contain the damage. Transparent communication. Keep team focused.
4
Recovery
Stabilize operations. Rebuild trust with stakeholders.
5
Post-Crisis Learning
Review what happened. Update plans. Build resilience for the future.
2023/24 Q5 — CEO facing social media crisis: (1) Acknowledge immediately, (2) Take responsibility, (3) Communicate transparently, (4) Take concrete corrective action, (5) Rebuild trust through follow-through.

Kotter's 8-Step Change Model ⭐ 2020/21 Q6a

"Very Carly Formed Vision, Enables Winning Great Strides"
Urgency · Coalition · Vision · Communicate · Empower · Wins · Gains · Sustain
1
Create a Sense of Urgency
Show people why change is necessary now. Use data, stories, market realities.
2
Build a Guiding Coalition
Assemble a team with enough power and credibility to lead the change.
3
Form a Strategic Vision
Create a clear, compelling vision of the future state.
4
Enlist a Volunteer Army / Communicate the Vision
Use every channel to communicate the vision. Get buy-in widely.
5
Enable Action by Removing Barriers
Remove obstacles — policies, structures, people resisting. Empower broad-based action.
6
Generate Short-Term Wins
Plan for visible wins early. Recognize and reward contributors. Builds momentum.
7
Sustain Acceleration
Don't declare victory too early. Use credibility from wins to drive more change.
8
Institute Change / Anchor in Culture
Make new behaviors the norm. Connect change to organizational success. Ensure successors embody the change.

Kurt Lewin's Change Model (simpler)

Unfreeze
Break down current behaviors and attitudes. Create motivation for change. Overcome resistance.
Change
Implement the new behaviors, processes, structures. Learning and transition period.
Refreeze
Stabilize the new state. Reinforce changes through culture, processes, rewards until they become the new normal.

Building a Crisis-Resilient Culture (2023/24 Q5b)

  • Psychological safety — people must feel safe to report problems early without fear
  • Scenario planning & crisis drills — practice prepares teams to act under pressure
  • Clear communication protocols — who communicates what to whom in a crisis
  • Trust-based relationships — teams that trust each other work faster in crises
  • Learning from near-misses — treat every small failure as a learning opportunity
  • Empowerment at all levels — people closest to problems must have authority to act

2023/24 Q1a (7 marks) — LMX Theory: Sarala's relationships with team members
LMX theory describes how leaders develop different quality relationships with different followers.

In-Group (High LMX) — Sithum & Malani: High trust, informal communication, given autonomy and challenging projects. They go above and beyond. Sarala seeks their input on key decisions. This is characteristic of in-group LMX — expanded roles and psychological closeness.

Mid-Group — Kalani & Sudesh: Formal, task-oriented communication. Complete assigned tasks adequately but don't volunteer extra. Clear instructions, monitored progress. Contract-based relationship (out-group LMX tendencies).

Out-Group (Low LMX) — Priya: Newest member, brief & basic interactions, focused only on task instructions. Limited engagement, declining confidence. Typical low LMX — minimal relationship investment.
2023/24 Q1b (8 marks) — Positive & negative consequences of differentiated LMX relationships
Positive: In-group members (Sithum, Malani) are highly motivated → better output. Leader efficiently allocates complex work to trusted members. High-LMX relationships create loyalty and commitment that benefits the organization.

Negative: Priya's confidence is wavering — she feels disconnected and less engaged. Kalani and Sudesh may feel under-valued compared to in-group. Perceived favoritism damages team cohesion. Risk of losing talent from out-group who are never developed. Ethical concern: unconscious bias may determine who gets in-group treatment. Long-term, this reduces the team's overall capacity.
2023/24 Q2a (8 marks) — Level 5 leaders: paradoxical blend of professional will and personal humility
Jim Collins (2001) found in his "Good to Great" research that the defining factor separating great from merely good companies was the presence of a Level 5 leader.

Professional Will: Fierce, unwavering resolve. Makes difficult decisions (e.g., Darwin Smith sold Kimberly-Clark's core mills). Sets extremely high standards. Takes full responsibility when things go wrong — "looks in the mirror." Builds for sustained greatness, not short-term gains.

Personal Humility: Avoids spotlight. Credits success to others and circumstances — "looks out the window." Modest, quiet, calm. Never boastful. Develops successors who may surpass them.

Why paradoxical: Western culture associates great leadership with charisma and ego. Level 5 leaders appear ordinary but drive extraordinary results precisely because their ego serves the organization, not themselves. The paradox is: the less they care about personal glory, the more enduring their impact.
2023/24 Q2b (7 marks) — Servant leadership as a contradiction to traditional leadership
Traditional leadership: Leader at the top of the hierarchy. Followers serve the leader's goals. Leader directs, followers execute. Control is centralized in the leader.

Servant leadership (Greenleaf, 1977): Inverts this model. Leader's primary purpose is to serve followers and help them achieve their full potential. Control is centered in followers (very high autonomy). The leader asks: "How can I help you succeed?" not "How can you help me succeed?"

Why it's a contradiction: (1) Power flows upward to followers, not down from leader. (2) Leader measures success by follower growth, not by leader's authority or recognition. (3) "Serve first, lead second" is the opposite of traditional leader-as-commander models.

But not fully a contradiction: Servant leaders still provide vision, strategic direction, and accountability — they just do so while prioritizing follower wellbeing. Nelson Mandela showed this balance.
2023/24 Q3a (8 marks) — Hersey-Blanchard model: adjusting style to follower readiness
The Hersey-Blanchard Situational Leadership model argues there is no one best leadership style. Effective leaders adapt their style to match the follower's readiness level (combination of ability and willingness).

R1 → S1 (Telling): Unable & unwilling follower. Leader provides specific, one-way direction, close supervision. Example: brand new employee in a technical role.
R2 → S2 (Selling/Coaching): Unable but willing. Two-way dialogue, explain why, build confidence. Example: enthusiastic new hire who lacks skills.
R3 → S3 (Participating/Supporting): Able but insecure. Follower-made decisions, leader listens, builds confidence. Example: experienced employee who has lost motivation.
R4 → S4 (Delegating): Able, confident, willing. Hand over full responsibility. Light supervision. Example: Jagath Perera's engineers (2020/21 Q2) — gave them freedom to manage their own KPIs.
2019/20 Q3 — Fiedler's Contingency Model: HR Manager scenario (LPC = 45)
LPC score of 45 = Task-motivated leader (low LPC, below 57).

Situational favorability analysis:
• Leader-member relations: POOR (employees are resisting the new policy — low trust)
• Task structure: LOW (new rewards policy is novel, not clearly structured)
• Position power: WEAK (external hire, new to the organization, not yet established)
→ Situation: Very unfavorable

Will the manager succeed? According to Fiedler, a task-motivated (low LPC) leader actually performs BEST in very unfavorable situations. So theoretically yes, this leader's task focus aligns with the situation. However, the strong employee resistance is a practical barrier — even with the right leader-situation match, the organizational dynamics are challenging.

Should the company make changes? The company should provide support through change management — help the manager build leader-member relations, communicate the rationale for the policy clearly, and involve employees in refinement of the reward system to reduce resistance.
2019/20 Q2iii — Hersey-Blanchard: team where all know their job well and want autonomy
This team is at Readiness Level R4 — members are highly able (know their job well), confident, and willing (make their own decisions, seek feedback independently).

The appropriate style is S4 — Delegating. The leader should:
• Hand over full task responsibility to team members
• Allow follower-made decisions with minimal intervention
• Provide relatively light supervision
• Be available when help is sought but not proactively direct
• Reinforce results and celebrate achievements

This matches Jagath Perera's approach in the Big W International case — "My job is to stay out of your way so you can do your work."

2023/24 Q5a (8 marks) — CEO handling sudden PR crisis from social media post
Steps as crisis leader:
1. Assess immediately — understand the full situation before responding
2. Take responsibility — acknowledge the issue publicly; do not deflect or minimize
3. Communicate transparently — issue a clear statement; explain what happened and what steps are being taken
4. Isolate the source — address the junior executive appropriately through HR processes
5. Engage affected parties — reach out directly to those hurt by the post
6. Demonstrate empathy — show genuine concern for those affected
7. Implement corrective policy — update social media guidelines and approvals
8. Monitor and follow through — track how the response lands; adjust communications

Key principle: "Never waste a crisis" — use it to demonstrate the organization's true values.
2020/21 Q6a (5 marks) — Kotter's 8-Step Model and facilitating change
Kotter argues that 70% of change efforts fail due to lack of urgency, insufficient coalition, or premature declaration of victory. The 8 steps provide a systematic process:

1. Create urgency (show the burning platform) → 2. Build coalition (get key supporters) → 3. Form vision (clear compelling future) → 4. Communicate widely → 5. Remove barriers → 6. Generate quick wins (build momentum) → 7. Sustain acceleration → 8. Anchor in culture (make it stick).

Effective leadership facilitates change by moving through all 8 steps — skipping steps leads to failure. The leader's role is most crucial at Steps 1 (urgency) and 8 (embedding in culture).
